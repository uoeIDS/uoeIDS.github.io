Exercise 8

Question: Recreate the following plot (see the online worksheet), and interpret what you see in context of the data.

At least up to a certain point, there seems to be a non-linear positive relationship between plastic waste per capita and the proportion of the population that leave near the coast, though with increasing variability in the plastic waste as the coastal population increases. In particular it is possible for countries still to have low values of plastic waste per capita even with large coastal populations relative to their size. The increasing relationship seems to break down for values of coastal population proportion bigger than 1; however, there seems to be a data quality issue here (more than 100% of the population live near the coast, which doesnt make sense).

In terms of differentiation by continent, its hard to detect much clustering visually. With one exception, South American countries appear relatively close together (moderate coastal population, moderate plastic waste), as do Oceanian countries (high coastal popularion, moderate plastic waste). Although they do not form a cluster, the majority of the countries with low plastic waste appear to be African countries with low coastal populations.

plastic_waste_edit <- plastic_waste %>% 
  mutate(coastal_pop_prop = coastal_pop / total_pop) %>%
  filter(plastic_waste_per_cap < 3)

ggplot(data = plastic_waste_edit, 
       mapping = aes(x = coastal_pop_prop, 
                     y = plastic_waste_per_cap, 
                     color = continent)) + 
    geom_point() +
    geom_smooth(color = "black") +
    scale_color_viridis_d() +
    labs(x = "Coastal population proportion (Coastal / total population)", 
         y = "Plastic waste per capita ", 
         color = "Continent",
         title = "Plastic waste vs. coastal population proportion",
         subtitle = "by continent") +
    theme_minimal()
